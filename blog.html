Q1 2026 • Transformation strategy

Why most transformations fail at execution — not strategy

The most common question we hear from leadership teams is some version of: “We’ve invested heavily in this — why isn’t it working?” The strategy is sound. The intent is genuine. The budget was approved. And yet the results aren’t there.

The answer, almost universally, is execution architecture. The gap between strategy and delivery isn’t filled by good intentions — it’s filled by structures, workflows, accountability mechanisms, and governance systems. When those don’t exist or don’t work, even the best strategy stalls.

What execution breakdown actually looks like

  • Teams working hard but from different priorities — no single operating rhythm
  • Decisions being made without reliable data, or the same decision made twice by different people
  • Change programs that produce activity but not measurable outcome
  • Performance that’s inconsistent across sites, regions, or functions without a clear reason why

What the fix looks like

It’s not more process. It’s the right structure — a single operating model that connects strategy to delivery through clear accountability, defined workflows, and consistent measurement. The organisations that get this right deliver faster, cost less, and scale more reliably.

Q1 2026 • Digital enablement

Where automation actually delivers ROI — and where it doesn’t

Automation is powerful when it’s applied to the right problems. It’s expensive and demoralising when it’s applied to the wrong ones. In our experience, the difference comes down to one question: is the process stable enough to automate?

If a process is manual because it’s genuinely complex and variable — because it requires human judgement at each step — automation won’t help. But if a process is manual because it’s always been manual — because it was designed before automation tools existed — the opportunity is significant. Most invoice processing, approval routing, compliance documentation, and reporting workflows fall into this category.

The questions to ask before you automate

  • Is this process stable and well-defined enough to codify?
  • How often does it run, and what does the manual cost actually add up to?
  • What’s the error rate, and what does each error cost?
  • Will the people affected by the change adopt the new system?

Common mistakes

  • Automating a broken process — you’ll get broken outcomes, faster
  • Building automation that the team doesn’t trust and works around
  • Underestimating the integration work required to make it actually connect
  • No handover plan — automation that only the person who built it can maintain

Q1 2026 • Procurement

Procurement transformation: moving from reactive to strategic

Most procurement functions are designed to process demand, not to manage it. They exist to approve and execute purchases, not to challenge them, shape them, or create value from them. The result is a function that’s busy, expensive, and adding less value than it should.

The organisations that have transformed procurement into a strategic function share a few characteristics: they have visibility of spend, they have accountability for outcomes, and they’ve structured vendor relationships around performance rather than just price.

Where to start

  • Get full spend visibility before making any structural changes — you need to know what you’re working with
  • Identify the vendor categories that represent the most cost, risk, or cycle time — those are the priorities
  • Look at approval workflows — most organisations have built sequential approvals that add no value
  • Review commercial terms — in most organisations, at least some contracts haven’t been renegotiated in years

What strategic procurement actually looks like

  • Forward visibility of demand that enables proactive sourcing
  • Vendor performance managed against agreed SLAs, not just cost
  • Approval workflows that are fast, auditable, and trusted
  • Commercial terms that reflect the organisation’s actual leverage

Q1 2026 • Operating models

Building operating models that actually scale

Most operating models are designed for the organisation as it is today. They break when the organisation grows, expands into new geographies, or absorbs an acquisition. The signs are usually familiar: inconsistency across sites, decision-making that’s slower as the organisation gets bigger, and performance that degrades at the edges.

A scalable operating model is designed around principles, not just current structure. It defines how decisions are made, how accountability flows, and how performance is measured — in a way that holds across different sizes, geographies, and operating contexts.

What makes an operating model scalable

  • Clear decision rights that don’t depend on specific people — the structure works even when individuals change
  • Standardised core processes that can be deployed consistently across new sites or regions
  • Performance measurement that gives leadership visibility without requiring manual aggregation
  • Governance that is light enough not to slow things down, but strong enough to maintain consistency

What breaks scalability

  • Operating models designed around the current leadership team’s preferences
  • Inconsistent process execution — every site doing things differently in ways that compound over time
  • Reporting that depends on people manually pulling data together rather than systems surfacing it automatically
